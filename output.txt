<p><b>Geography</b> is the branch of <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> that studies the lands, the features, the inhabitants, and the phenomena of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">2</a></sup> A literal translation would be "to describe or write about the Earth". The first person to use the word "geography" was <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">Eratosthenes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">3</a></sup> Four historical traditions in geographical research are <a href="/wiki/Spatial_analysis" title="Spatial analysis">spatial analysis</a> of the natural and the human phenomena, <a href="/wiki/Area_studies" title="Area studies">area studies</a>, study of the man-land relationship, and research in the <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">Earth sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">4</a></sup> Nonetheless, modern geography is an all-encompassing discipline that foremost seeks to understand the Earth and all of its human and natural complexities - not merely where objects are, but how they have changed and come to be. Geography has been called "the world discipline" and "the bridge between the human and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_science" title="Physical science">physical science</a>". Geography is divided into two main branches: <a href="/wiki/Human_geography" title="Human geography">human geography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">physical geography</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">5</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">6</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">7</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Knowledge</b> is a familiarity with someone or something, which can include <a href="/wiki/Fact" title="Fact">facts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a>, <a href="/wiki/Description" title="Description">descriptions</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skills" title="Skills">skills</a> acquired through <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">experience</a> or <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>. It can refer to the theoretical or practical understanding of a subject. It can be implicit or explicit; it can be more or less formal or systematic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">1</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, the study of knowledge is called <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>; the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> famously defined knowledge as "<a href="/wiki/Justified_true_belief" title="Justified true belief">justified true belief</a>." However, no single agreed upon definition of knowledge exists, though there are numerous theories to explain it.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, <b>necessity</b> and <b>sufficiency</b> are implicational relationships between <a href="/wiki/Statement_(logic)" title="Statement">statements</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[]</sup> The assertion that one statement is a  <a href="/wiki/Condition_(philosophy)" title="Condition">condition</a> of another means that the former statement is true <a href="/wiki/If_and_only_if" title="If and only if">if and only if</a> the latter is true. That is, the two statements must be either simultaneously true or simultaneously false. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordinary_English" title="Ordinary English">ordinary English</a>, ‘necessary’ and ‘sufficient’ indicate relations between <a href="/wiki/Condition_(philosophy)" title="Condition">conditions</a> or states of affairs, not statements. Being male sibling is a necessary and sufficient condition for being a brother. Fred’s being a male sibling is necessary and sufficient for the truth of the statement that Fred is a brother.</p>

<p>A <b>quality</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">1</a></sup> is an attribute or a <a href="/wiki/Property_(philosophy)" title="Property">property</a>. Attributes are ascribable, by a subject, whereas properties are possessible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cargile.2C_1995_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cargile.2C_1995-2">2</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[]</sup> In contemporary <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> the idea of qualities, and especially how to distinguish certain kinds of qualities from one another, remains controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cargile.2C_1995_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cargile.2C_1995-2">2</a></sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Modern_philosophy" title="Modern philosophy">modern philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, a <b>property</b> is an attribute of an <a href="/wiki/Object_(philosophy)" title="Object">object</a>; a red object is said to have the property of redness. The property may be considered a form of object in its own right, able to possess other properties. A property however differs from individual objects in that it may be instantiated, and often in more than one thing. It differs from the logical/mathematical concept of <a href="/wiki/Class_(set_theory)" title="Class">class</a> by not having any concept of <a href="/wiki/Extensionality" title="Extensionality">extensionality</a>, and from the philosophical concept of <a href="/wiki/Class_(philosophy)" title="Class">class</a> in that a property is considered to be distinct from the objects which possess it. Understanding how different individual entities can in some sense have some of the same properties is the basis of the <a href="/wiki/Problem_of_universals" title="Problem of universals">problem of universals</a>. The terms <b>attribute</b> and <b>quality</b> have similar meanings.</p>

<p>A <b>quality</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">1</a></sup> is an attribute or a <a href="/wiki/Property_(philosophy)" title="Property">property</a>. Attributes are ascribable, by a subject, whereas properties are possessible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cargile.2C_1995_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cargile.2C_1995-2">2</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[]</sup> In contemporary <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> the idea of qualities, and especially how to distinguish certain kinds of qualities from one another, remains controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cargile.2C_1995_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cargile.2C_1995-2">2</a></sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Modern_philosophy" title="Modern philosophy">modern philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, a <b>property</b> is an attribute of an <a href="/wiki/Object_(philosophy)" title="Object">object</a>; a red object is said to have the property of redness. The property may be considered a form of object in its own right, able to possess other properties. A property however differs from individual objects in that it may be instantiated, and often in more than one thing. It differs from the logical/mathematical concept of <a href="/wiki/Class_(set_theory)" title="Class">class</a> by not having any concept of <a href="/wiki/Extensionality" title="Extensionality">extensionality</a>, and from the philosophical concept of <a href="/wiki/Class_(philosophy)" title="Class">class</a> in that a property is considered to be distinct from the objects which possess it. Understanding how different individual entities can in some sense have some of the same properties is the basis of the <a href="/wiki/Problem_of_universals" title="Problem of universals">problem of universals</a>. The terms <b>attribute</b> and <b>quality</b> have similar meanings.</p>

<p>Economics is the social science that studies how rational individuals, groups, and organizations, manage scarce resources, which have alternative uses, to achieve desirable ends. Therefore, we can say that there is an economic problem, subject of study of the economic science, whenever scarce resources, which have alternative uses, are employed, and a decision is made by the resources controlling players, to attain the best possible outcome under bounded rational conditions, in other words, to maximize value subject to the constrains imposed by the information the agents have, the cognitive limitations of their minds, and the finite amount of time they have to make a decision. On the one hand, the continuous interplay done by these economic actors in all markets set the prices for all goods and services which make the rational managing of scarce resources possible in the first place. And on the other hand, the decisions made by the same actors, while pursuing their own interest, determine the level of output, consumption, savings, and investment in an economy, as well as the remuneration paid to the owners of labor, capital and land.</p>

<p><b>Greek</b> is an independent branch of the <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> family of languages. Native to the southern <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>, western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Islands" title="Aegean Islands">Aegean Islands</a>, it has the longest documented history of any Indo-European language, spanning 34 centuries of written records. Its writing system has been the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek alphabet</a> for the majority of its history; other systems, such as <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cypriot_syllabary" title="Cypriot syllabary">Cypriot syllabary</a>, were previously used. The alphabet arose from the <a href="/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet" title="Phoenician alphabet">Phoenician script</a> and was in turn the basis of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coptic_alphabet" title="Coptic alphabet">Coptic</a>, and many other writing systems.</p>

<p>The <b>tree of life</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a> used to describe the relationships between organisms, both living and extinct. Its use dates back to at least the early 1800s. It was employed by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> to express the concept of the branching divergence of varieties and then species in a process of <a href="/wiki/Common_descent" title="Common descent">common descent</a> from ancestors. <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Haeckel" title="Ernst Haeckel">Ernst Haeckel</a> coined the term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogeny" title="Phylogeny">phylogeny</a> for the evolutionary relationships of species through time, and went further than Darwin in proposing phylogenic histories of life. The modern development of this idea is called the <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_tree" title="Phylogenetic tree">phylogenetic tree</a>.</p>

<p>A <b>metaphor</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_speech" title="Figure of speech">figure of speech</a> that describes a subject by asserting that it is, on some point of comparison, the same as another otherwise unrelated object. Metaphor is a type of <a href="/wiki/Analogy" title="Analogy">analogy</a> and is closely related to other <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetorical</a> <a href="/wiki/Literal_and_figurative_language" title="Literal and figurative language">figures of speech</a> that achieve their effects via association, comparison or resemblance including <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyperbole" title="Hyperbole">hyperbole</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Simile" title="Simile">simile</a>.</p>
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figure_of_speech
<p>A <b>figure of speech</b> is the use of a <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">word</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">phrase</a>, which transcends its literal interpretation. It can be a special repetition, arrangement or omission of words with literal meaning, or a <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">phrase</a> with a specialized meaning not based on the literal meaning of the words in it, as in <a href="/wiki/Idiom" title="Idiom">idiom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Simile" title="Simile">simile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyperbole" title="Hyperbole">hyperbole</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personification" title="Personification">personification</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Synecdoche" title="Synecdoche">synecdoche</a>. Figures of speech often provide emphasis, freshness of expression, or clarity. However, clarity may also suffer from their use, as any figure of speech introduces an ambiguity between literal and figurative interpretation. A figure of speech is sometimes called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhetorical_figure" title="Rhetorical figure">rhetorical figure</a> or a locution.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, a <b>word</b> is the smallest element that may be uttered in isolation with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semantic" title="Semantic">semantic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">pragmatic</a> content" title="Meaning">meaning</a>). This contrasts with a <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">morpheme</a>, which is the smallest unit of meaning but will not necessarily stand on its own. A word may consist of a single morpheme, or several, whereas a morpheme may not be able to stand on its own as a word. A complex word will typically include a <a href="/wiki/Root_(linguistics)" title="Root">root</a> and one or more <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">affixes</a>, or more than one root in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_word" title="Compound word">compound</a>. Words can be put together to build larger elements of <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">phrases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clause" title="Clause">clauses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence">sentences</a>.</p>

<p><b>Science</b> is a systematic enterprise that builds and organizes <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> in the form of testable explanations and predictions about the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilson_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-wilson-2">2</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">3</a></sup> In an older and closely related meaning, "science" also refers to a body of knowledge itself, of the type that can be rationally explained and reliably applied. <p><b>Knowledge</b> is a familiarity with someone or something, which can include <a href="/wiki/Fact" title="Fact">facts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a>, <a href="/wiki/Description" title="Description">descriptions</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skills" title="Skills">skills</a> acquired through <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">experience</a> or <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>. It can refer to the theoretical or practical understanding of a subject. It can be implicit or explicit; it can be more or less formal or systematic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">1</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, the study of knowledge is called <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>; the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> famously defined knowledge as "<a href="/wiki/Justified_true_belief" title="Justified true belief">justified true belief</a>." However, no single agreed upon definition of knowledge exists, though there are numerous theories to explain it.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, <b>necessity</b> and <b>sufficiency</b> are implicational relationships between <a href="/wiki/Statement_(logic)" title="Statement">statements</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[]</sup> The assertion that one statement is a  <a href="/wiki/Condition_(philosophy)" title="Condition">condition</a> of another means that the former statement is true <a href="/wiki/If_and_only_if" title="If and only if">if and only if</a> the latter is true. That is, the two statements must be either simultaneously true or simultaneously false. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordinary_English" title="Ordinary English">ordinary English</a>, ‘necessary’ and ‘sufficient’ indicate relations between <a href="/wiki/Condition_(philosophy)" title="Condition">conditions</a> or states of affairs, not statements. Being male sibling is a necessary and sufficient condition for being a brother. Fred’s being a male sibling is necessary and sufficient for the truth of the statement that Fred is a brother.</p>

<p>A <b>quality</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">1</a></sup> is an attribute or a <a href="/wiki/Property_(philosophy)" title="Property">property</a>. Attributes are ascribable, by a subject, whereas properties are possessible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cargile.2C_1995_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cargile.2C_1995-2">2</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[]</sup> In contemporary <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> the idea of qualities, and especially how to distinguish certain kinds of qualities from one another, remains controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cargile.2C_1995_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cargile.2C_1995-2">2</a></sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Modern_philosophy" title="Modern philosophy">modern philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, a <b>property</b> is an attribute of an <a href="/wiki/Object_(philosophy)" title="Object">object</a>; a red object is said to have the property of redness. The property may be considered a form of object in its own right, able to possess other properties. A property however differs from individual objects in that it may be instantiated, and often in more than one thing. It differs from the logical/mathematical concept of <a href="/wiki/Class_(set_theory)" title="Class">class</a> by not having any concept of <a href="/wiki/Extensionality" title="Extensionality">extensionality</a>, and from the philosophical concept of <a href="/wiki/Class_(philosophy)" title="Class">class</a> in that a property is considered to be distinct from the objects which possess it. Understanding how different individual entities can in some sense have some of the same properties is the basis of the <a href="/wiki/Problem_of_universals" title="Problem of universals">problem of universals</a>. The terms <b>attribute</b> and <b>quality</b> have similar meanings.</p>

<p><b>Politics</b> is the practice and theory of influencing other people on a civic or individual level. More narrowly, it refers to achieving and exercising positions of <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> — organized control over a human community, particularly a <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State">state</a>. A variety of methods are employed in politics, which include promoting one's own political views among people, <a href="/wiki/Negotiation" title="Negotiation">negotiation</a> with other political subjects, making <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">laws</a>, and exercising <a href="/wiki/Force_(law)" title="Force">force</a>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warfare" title="Warfare">warfare</a> against adversaries. Politics is exercised on a wide range of social levels, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clans" title="Clans">clans</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tribes" title="Tribes">tribes</a> of traditional societies, through modern <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local governments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Company" title="Company">companies</a> and institutions up to <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign states</a>, to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_politics" title="International politics">international level</a>.</p>

<p>To distinguish the term  from govern: "governance" is the concrete activity that reproduces a formal or informal organization. The discipline Terminology studies among other things how such terms of art come to be and their interrelationships within a culture. Terminology differs from <a href="/wiki/Lexicography" title="Lexicography">lexicography</a> in studying concepts, conceptual systems, and their labels, whereas lexicography study words and their meanings.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, a <b>word</b> is the smallest element that may be uttered in isolation with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semantic" title="Semantic">semantic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">pragmatic</a> content" title="Meaning">meaning</a>). This contrasts with a <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">morpheme</a>, which is the smallest unit of meaning but will not necessarily stand on its own. A word may consist of a single morpheme, or several, whereas a morpheme may not be able to stand on its own as a word. A complex word will typically include a <a href="/wiki/Root_(linguistics)" title="Root">root</a> and one or more <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">affixes</a>, or more than one root in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_word" title="Compound word">compound</a>. Words can be put together to build larger elements of <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">phrases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clause" title="Clause">clauses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence">sentences</a>.</p>

<p><b>Linguistics</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">1</a></sup> study of <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">2</a></sup> There are broadly three aspects to the study, which include language form, language meaning, and language in context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">3</a></sup> The earliest known activities in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descriptive_linguistics" title="Descriptive linguistics">description of language</a> have been attributed to <a href="/wiki/P%C4%81%E1%B9%87ini" title="Pāṇini">Pāṇini</a> around 500 BCE, with his analysis of <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> in .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">4</a></sup></p>
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science
<p><b>Science</b> is a systematic enterprise that builds and organizes <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> in the form of testable explanations and predictions about the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilson_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-wilson-2">2</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">3</a></sup> In an older and closely related meaning, "science" also refers to a body of knowledge itself, of the type that can be rationally explained and reliably applied. A practitioner of science is known as a <a href="/wiki/Scientist" title="Scientist">scientist</a>.</p>
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge
<p><b>Knowledge</b> is a familiarity with someone or something, which can include <a href="/wiki/Fact" title="Fact">facts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a>, <a href="/wiki/Description" title="Description">descriptions</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skills" title="Skills">skills</a> acquired through <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">experience</a> or <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>. It can refer to the theoretical or practical understanding of a subject. It can be implicit or explicit; it can be more or less formal or systematic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">1</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, the study of knowledge is called <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>; the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> famously defined knowledge as "<a href="/wiki/Justified_true_belief" title="Justified true belief">justified true belief</a>." However, no single agreed upon definition of knowledge exists, though there are numerous theories to explain it.</p>
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fact
<p>A <b>fact</b> is something that has really occurred or is actually the case. The usual test for a statement of fact is verifiability, that is whether it can be <a href="/wiki/Proof_(truth)" title="Proof">proven</a> to correspond to <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">experience</a>. Standard reference works are often used to check facts. <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">Scientific</a> facts are verified by repeatable <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experiments</a>.</p>

<p>A <b>proof</b> is <a href="/wiki/Necessity_and_sufficiency" title="Necessity and sufficiency">sufficient</a> <a href="/wiki/Evidence" title="Evidence">evidence</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Argument" title="Argument">argument</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">truth</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Proposition" title="Proposition">proposition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">1</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">2</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">3</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">4</a></sup></p>
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necessity_and_sufficiency
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, <b>necessity</b> and <b>sufficiency</b> are implicational relationships between <a href="/wiki/Statement_(logic)" title="Statement">statements</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[]</sup> The assertion that one statement is a  <a href="/wiki/Condition_(philosophy)" title="Condition">condition</a> of another means that the former statement is true <a href="/wiki/If_and_only_if" title="If and only if">if and only if</a> the latter is true. That is, the two statements must be either simultaneously true or simultaneously false. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordinary_English" title="Ordinary English">ordinary English</a>, ‘necessary’ and ‘sufficient’ indicate relations between <a href="/wiki/Condition_(philosophy)" title="Condition">conditions</a> or states of affairs, not statements. Being male sibling is a necessary and sufficient condition for being a brother. Fred’s being a male sibling is necessary and sufficient for the truth of the statement that Fred is a brother.</p>

<p><b>Logic</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-argumentative_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-argumentative-1">1</a></sup> has two meanings: first, it describes the use of valid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reasoning" title="Reasoning">reasoning</a> in some activity; second, it names the <a href="/wiki/Normative" title="Normative">normative</a> study of reasoning or a branch thereof.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopkinStroll1993_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-PopkinStroll1993-2">2</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jacquette2002_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-jacquette2002-3">3</a></sup> In the latter sense, it features most prominently in the subjects of <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>.</p>
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reasoning
<p><b>Reason</b> is the capacity for <a href="/wiki/Consciousness" title="Consciousness">consciously</a> making sense of things, applying <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, for establishing and verifying <a href="/wiki/Fact" title="Fact">facts</a>, and changing or justifying practices, institutions, and <a href="/wiki/Belief" title="Belief">beliefs</a> based on new or existing information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">1</a></sup> It is closely associated with such characteristically <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> activities as <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a>, and is normally considered to be a definitive characteristic of <a href="/wiki/Human_nature" title="Human nature">human nature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">2</a></sup> The concept of reason is sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Rationality" title="Rationality">rationality</a> and sometimes as <b>discursive reason</b>, in opposition to <a href="/wiki/Intuition_(psychology)" title="Intuition">intuitive reason</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">3</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Consciousness</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Quality_(philosophy)" title="Quality">quality</a> or state of being aware of an external object or something within oneself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">1</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-van_Gulick2004_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-van_Gulick2004-2">2</a></sup> It has been defined as: <a href="/wiki/Sentience" title="Sentience">sentience</a>, <a href="/wiki/Awareness" title="Awareness">awareness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subjectivity" title="Subjectivity">subjectivity</a>, the ability to <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">experience</a> or to <a href="/wiki/Feeling" title="Feeling">feel</a>, wakefulness, having a sense of <a href="/wiki/Self" title="Self">selfhood</a>, and the executive control system of the mind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farthing1992Psychology_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Farthing1992Psychology-3">3</a></sup> Despite the difficulty in definition, many philosophers believe that there is a broadly shared underlying intuition about what consciousness is.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">4</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/Max_Velmans" title="Max Velmans">Max Velmans</a> and Susan Schneider wrote in : "Anything that we are aware of at a given moment forms part of our consciousness, making conscious experience at once the most familiar and most mysterious aspect of our lives."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">5</a></sup></p>

<p>A <b>quality</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">1</a></sup> is an attribute or a <a href="/wiki/Property_(philosophy)" title="Property">property</a>. Attributes are ascribable, by a subject, whereas properties are possessible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cargile.2C_1995_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cargile.2C_1995-2">2</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[]</sup> In contemporary <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> the idea of qualities, and especially how to distinguish certain kinds of qualities from one another, remains controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cargile.2C_1995_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cargile.2C_1995-2">2</a></sup></p>
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_(philosophy)
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Modern_philosophy" title="Modern philosophy">modern philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, a <b>property</b> is an attribute of an <a href="/wiki/Object_(philosophy)" title="Object">object</a>; a red object is said to have the property of redness. The property may be considered a form of object in its own right, able to possess other properties. A property however differs from individual objects in that it may be instantiated, and often in more than one thing. It differs from the logical/mathematical concept of <a href="/wiki/Class_(set_theory)" title="Class">class</a> by not having any concept of <a href="/wiki/Extensionality" title="Extensionality">extensionality</a>, and from the philosophical concept of <a href="/wiki/Class_(philosophy)" title="Class">class</a> in that a property is considered to be distinct from the objects which possess it. Understanding how different individual entities can in some sense have some of the same properties is the basis of the <a href="/wiki/Problem_of_universals" title="Problem of universals">problem of universals</a>. The terms <b>attribute</b> and <b>quality</b> have similar meanings.</p>
